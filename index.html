Stories of Salt

Abstract

The word Culture in Latin means nothing else than “to cultivate”. The meaning of cultivation within Sandor Ellix Kutz’s book The Art of Fermentation, “… constitutes the totality of all that humans seek to pass from generation to generation, including language, music, art, literature, scientific knowledge, and belief systems, as well as agriculture and culinary techniques (in both of which fermentation occupies a central role).”

In conclusion cultivation cannot happen if there is no one following practices of our ancestors. In order for them to be continued they need to be transferred and passed on from one generation to another. Rituals and traditions and the cultivation of those would mean nothing without preservation. In my thesis, this act of conversation will be facilitated through the topic of the preservation of food through salt and the practice of fermentation. This includes history, fables, cultural folktales and oral storytelling. The topic is to rediscover for myself and others, who feel lost and follow the wish to be connected, ways in which we can reconnect to what we call home. A recipe for Heimweh that can connect us back to our roots. How can salt within its conservational qualities within food preparation be used to connect and preserve the ties to our roots? How can it become a material agent to preserve roots and culture and practices of cooking and the culture of food? To be able to spark this conversation I am turning to the books Salt by Mark Kurlansky, The Art of Fermentation by Sandor Ellix Katz, Under the Jaguar Sun by Italo Calvino, Braiding Sweetgrass by Robin Wall Kimmerer and Mi país inventado by Isabel Allende. I use my own memory to reflect on conversations that I had with the artist Asli Hatipoğlu and friends and family members. Using not only academic sources but as well historical sources, fables, cultural folktales, oral storytelling. Finally, my practice in the kitchen complements the work as practical research.
